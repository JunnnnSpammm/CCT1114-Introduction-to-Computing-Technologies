Game Design and Development

Game design and development is the process of creating and shaping the mechanics, systems and rules of a game. Games can be made for entertainment, education, exercise or experimental purposes using programming languages such as Python, Java, Javascript, C# and C++. However, the programming language that is mostly used by the industry is C++.

For Example:

  • World of Warcraft
  • Counter-Strike
  • Metro 2033
  • Assassin's Creed Unity
C++ is mainly used because of its flexibility and high abstraction level which allows you to have precise control over how a computer's hardware is interacting with the code that you write. C++ also provides more granular controls over how graphics are processed by the system, which plays a vital role in game design since it ensures the quality of the graphics as well as the performance and smoothness of the game. Another key benefit of using C++ is its unique ability to optimise special parts of your game design, giving them their abstractions. Essentially, it is akin to giving specific elements of the game their infrastructure and resources which would be difficult to implement if not impossible with many other languages.[1] Besides that, one of the most used game engines in the world to make games, are built with C++ in mind. Most gaming consoles are also built from the ground up using C++.

However, besides the choice of programming language, there are also many aspects that are involved in making a good game.

2. Game Mechanics

Game mechanics is the spirit of a game. It is how the player controls the game and consists of rules regarding the movement of player characters, environment allowances, and the general rules of a game. Game mechanics also include how a game reacts to player controls which also follow a set of rules.[3] The rules always refer to the type of game.

Besides game type, the other rules also important, such as:

  • The Space of the Game: The setting of the game
  • The State of the Game: This could include player score or health, current level, or other important game variables

3. Storytelling[7]

Storytelling has been one of the main factor of why a player would continue playing a game. Games with well-written story can make one's game truly shine and keep the players wanting more. Besides, a good story can improve the players experience throughout the whole game. Hence, it helps the players build an emotional connection with the world and characters.

Storytelling truly is a complex process. It involves various elements such as gameplay, narrative, and visual design. The use of storytelling in video games differs from other traditional forms of storytelling, like movies, books, or theatre. The player is an active participant in the story. He has power over the choices and actions of the protagonist.

The use of storytelling in video games differs from other traditional forms of storytelling, like movies, books, or theatre. The player is an active participant in the story. He has power over the choices and actions of the protagonist.

Benefits of Storytelling

One of the key benefits of storytelling is that it can provide players with a more immersive experience. It allows players to inhabit the roles of the characters; become fully invested in the game's narrative. The players get a sense of emotional attachment and investment in the outcome. It leads to a more satisfying experience.

Another benefit is that it can help reinforce important messages or themes. Whether it's exploring complex moral dilemmas or shining a light on social issues. Games can use their narratives to encourage players to think critically about the world around them. It can be particularly impactful for younger players. They may be more receptive to these messages when presented in a fun and engaging format.

Storytelling also encourages critical thinking and problem-solving skills. Many games require players to navigate through complex storylines, make difficult decisions, and solve puzzles to progress. It helps players develop such skills as :

  • Logical reasoning
  • Creative thinking
  • Adaptability to ever-changing scenarios that can be applied in the real world

Types of Narrative for Storytelling

As we have established, a well-crafted story can elevate a game from being just an entertaining experience. Therefore, we must know which practices of storytelling fits our game the most. There are different types of storytelling such as;

  1. Linear storytelling:
    It is the most traditional form. In linear storytelling, the game follows a set path that the player follows in a specific order. As a result, the player's actions have little to no effect on the story. This type of storytelling is common in strong narratives.

  2. Non-Linear Storytelling:
    This type of storytelling is a less structured approach. You don't get the game's story in a specific order, and the player's actions can significantly impact the story's outcome. This type of storytelling is common in open-world games.

  3. Emergent Storytelling:
    This type of storytelling is one of a kind and is the most creative approach. It is a type of storytelling that emerges from the gameplay itself. The player's actions and decisions create unique and unpredictable stories that are not part of the game's narrative. For example, Minecraft and The Sims use this type of storytelling.

Conclusion

In summary, storytelling is a complex and multi-layered process that involves various elements. It includes gameplay, narrative, visual design, character development and worldbuilding. It's a unique form of storytelling that allows players to be active participants in the story. There appears to be the potential to create a deep emotional bond between the player and the game world.

5. Game Pacing

Game Pacing is defined as an intentional control and variation of the tempo and intensity of gameplay to maintain player interest and satisfaction. It involves carefully balancing moments of challenge and relaxation, fast-paced action and slower exploration, and high and low stakes. By controlling the game's pace, developers can create a more immersive and enjoyable experience for gamers. Understanding game pacing is crucial in the gaming industry as it directly impacts player engagement and satisfaction. A well-paced game can keep players hooked for hours, leading to increased playtime and potentially higher revenue for game developers and publishers. Moreover, game pacing also influences the overall perception and reputation of a game, which can significantly impact sales and the success of a gaming business.

Significance of Game Pacing in Games[1]

Game pacing holds immense significance in the gaming industry due to several reasons. Firstly, it directly affects the player's level of immersion and enjoyment. A game that is too slow or monotonous can bore players, resulting in disengagement and abandonment. Conversely, a game that is too fast-paced or challenging can overwhelm players, leading to frustration and a negative gaming experience. Therefore, striking the right balance in game pacing is crucial for keeping players engaged and satisfied. Secondly, game pacing influences the overall narrative structure and storytelling in games. By manipulating the pace, developers can effectively build tension, create climactic moments, and deliver impactful plot twists. This enhances the emotional connection between players and the game, making the experience more memorable and captivating. Furthermore, game pacing also plays a role in player retention and replayability. A well-paced game can provide a sense of progression and accomplishment, motivating players to continue playing and exploring the game's world. Additionally, different pacing techniques can be employed to create different gameplay experiences, encouraging players to revisit the game and try alternative playstyles.

Who benefits from Game Pacing in Gaming?[10]

Various stakeholders in the gaming ecosystem benefit from understanding and implementing effective game pacing techniques.

  1. Gamers: Players are the primary beneficiaries of well-paced games. They can enjoy a more immersive and engaging experience that keeps them entertained and invested in the game.

  2. Game Developers: Understanding game pacing allows developers to create more compelling and marketable games. By crafting a well-paced experience, developers can attract a larger player base, increase player retention, and generate higher revenue.

  3. Publishers: Game publishers benefit from game pacing as it directly impacts the marketability and commercial success of a game. A well-paced game is more likely to receive positive reviews, generate word-of-mouth recommendations, and achieve higher sales.

  4. Streamers and Content Creators: Streamers and content creators rely on engaging and entertaining gameplay to attract viewers and build a following. Well-paced games provide them with exciting content to showcase and entertain their audience.

Best Practices when Considering Game Pacing in Gaming and Why it Matters[10]

Implementing effective game pacing requires careful consideration and adherence to best practices. For example:

  1. Understanding the Target Audience: Different player demographics have varying preferences when it comes to game pacing. By understanding the target audience's preferences and playstyles, businesses can tailor the game's pacing to cater to their needs and maximize player satisfaction.

  2. Player's Skill Level: One aspect of game pacing that is often overlooked is the player’s level of experience. Novice players will require a different pacing than experienced players. For example, in a strategy game  (For example: League of Legends, Dota 2, StarCraft II), a novice player might need more time to plan each move, while an experienced player can make decisions more quickly. The game’s pacing should be adjusted accordingly to accommodate different players individual skill levels.

  3. Game's Pace: Another important thing to account for when creating or developing a game's pace, is the genre that the game is based on. Action games or first-person shooters are typically fast-paced, while puzzle games are usually slower and requires more thought in each step. The pacing should match accordingly to the gameplay so that the players won't be left frustrated or bored of the game's speed.

  4. Provide Variety: Monotonous pacing can lead to player fatigue and disengagement. A variety of gameplay experiences should be included, such as action-packed sequences, exploration, puzzle-solving, and narrative-driven moments. Game Developers should alternate between these techniques so that the game's community and players don't get put off by the gameplay and stop playing.

  5. Player Feedback: Game pacing is subjective and can vary based on individual player preferences and their background. Businesses need to iterate and test the pacing during the game development process, gathering feedback from playtesters and making adjustments to enhance the overall experience. If playtesters are consistently complaining that the game is too slow or too fast, then the developers should put out patches for the game to adjust the pacing accordingly. Game Developers should also keep an eye out for player feedback after release so that patches can be sent out to settle problems within the game.

Example of Intensity Related to Gameplay Time

Conclusion

Game pacing is an important consideration for game developers to create an enjoyable experience for their players, create more compelling and marketable games, increase commercial success and attracts players and build a community. Different aspects such as understanding the target audience, considering player's skill levels, designing a game's pace that suits the gameplay, providing variety in the gameplay, deliberation over player feedback should all be taken into account when adjusting the pacing of a game.

6. Player Engagement[11]

Player engagement and player experience are closely interconnected in the realm of video games. Player engagement refers to the extent to which players are captivated, involved, and invested in a game. Player engagement is influenced by many different factors, including gameplay mechanics, progression systems, graphics, storytelling, content updates, and in-game events. However, an element that is often overlooked is the effect of inadequate player support and community management. When facing issues while playing a game, players expect them to be resolved quickly, either by getting help from fellow players in the community or by contacting player support. Gaming involves an emotional attachment which is hard to compare with any other product or service. Being unable to get an issue resolved not only leads to frustration and dissatisfaction, but it can severely diminish player engagement.

Best Practices for Player Engagement

  • Gameplay Mechanics: A Game Designer should ensure that players are engaged by incorporating a variety of gameplay mechanics, such as exploration, combat, and puzzles.

  • Story Immersion: Players should be given a reason to care about the game world and its characters. Otherwise, they will quickly lose interest. There are many ways of delivering immersion to the player: visual, auditory, narrative, constructive, and more.

  • Community Research: Game Designers need to understand your player’s/community’s needs, behaviours, and preferences. What do they look for in their gaming experience? Why do they respond to certain features and not others? What designs and colour schemes will keep them engaged? Finding answers to these questions can help you create a game they will love.

Effects of Having Disengaged Players

Having disengaged players can have several negative consequences for a game, affecting both its short-term and long-term success. The key consequences of having disengaged players:

  • Declining Player Retention: When disengaged, players are more likely to stop playing a game. This leads to a decline in player retention, meaning fewer active players over time. A shrinking player base can negatively impact the game’s ecosystem and overall success.

  • Reduced Monetization Opportunities: Disengaged players are less likely to spend money on in-game purchases, limiting monetization opportunities for the game. They may be less motivated to invest in cosmetic items, expansions, or premium features, resulting in decreased revenue generation for the game company.

  • Negative Word-of-mouth: Disengaged players who are dissatisfied with their experience may spread negative word-of-mouth about the game. This can deter potential new players from trying or purchasing the game. Negative reviews, social media posts, or forum discussions can significantly impact the game’s reputation and potential success, leading to lower sales and diminished market presence.

  • Poor Community Interaction: Disengaged players are less likely to actively participate in the game’s community. They may be less inclined to engage in discussions, provide feedback, or contribute positively to the community environment. This lack of involvement can result in a less vibrant and supportive community, affecting the overall social experience for other players.

  • Impact on Development Priorities: Disengaged players may not provide valuable feedback or suggestions for improving the game. This lack of input can hinder the development team’s understanding of player preferences and needs. Without insights from disengaged players, the game company may struggle to make informed decisions about updates, patches, and future content, potentially missing opportunities for improvement.

  • Missed Opportunities for Upselling and Expansion: Disengaged players who have already purchased the base game may be less likely to invest in additional expansions, DLCs, or sequels. This results in missed opportunities for upselling and expanding the game’s revenue streams. Disengagement can limit the game’s potential for ongoing monetization and growth.

Conclusion

In the world of video games, player engagement closely links to whether a game is considered successful or not. Applying the best practices for player engagement such as gameplay mechanics, story immersion and community research will allow the game to have a healthy player base and avoid effects of having a disengaged player base such as declining player retention, reduced monetization opportunities, negative word-of-mouth, player community interaction, impact on development priorities and missed opportunities for upselling and expansion.

We might take it for granted, but video games depend a lot on the player’s sensory experiences. Graphics are what usually grasp our attention first. Our eyes capture what the game looks like, how the world is built, how our character looks, and we can even determine the genre of game we’d be playing. Sound is the other main component in video games. This is why sound is so important to do it properly, it adds another layer of depth into the experience.

Best Practices for Developing Quality Graphics and Audio in Video Games

Good game design uses all of the available tools to create a well-rounded experience for the player.

  1. It is important to consider the player’s perspective. What kind of graphics and audio will create the most immersive experience for the player? For example: background noise succeeds well in creating an immersive experience for players.

  2. The game’s overall aesthetic must be considered. The graphics and audio should complement each other to create a cohesive look and feel for the game.

  3. Graphics and audio can be used to convey important information to the player. For example, in horror games such as Phasmophobia visual and auditorial clues are given to the players to figure out what kind of ghost is haunting a certain location.

When used effectively, graphics and audio can enhance the player’s experience and make the game more enjoyable. However, it is important to consider all aspects of the game design before making decisions about graphics and audio.

Effects of Good Graphics and Audio Utilisation

  • Immersion: Bringing the game world to life will immerse the player in it. In certain games, the footstep sounds vary, depending on the surface you are going through. Small details like this can add a lot to the player's experience. Immersion is the result of a great atmosphere creation. In consequence, the player goes a layer deeper into the experience and feels they’re part of the world. The graphics and sound design team must be congruent with the type of world created. Imagine playing Dead Space without blood spatters and the sound assets replaced with ones that players would hear in Mario. Instead of conveying a horrific feeling, the game would be perceived as comedic, and satirical, to say the least.

  • Emotional Response: Once players are fully immersed in the video game, they are more susceptible to providing an emotional response, depending on the situation they’ve been put into. It can be as simple as a cutscene where the character says goodbye to a loved one, or a gruesome scene where the main character is tied up and something is slowly lurking towards them. Adding the correct characteristics to a cutscene might make players feel a certain way. They can be sad, disgusted, happy, melancholic, and feel so many other responses at the same time. The purpose of sound is to further complement the experience, and emotional responses are an essential part of the gaming loop. They can play a huge part in bringing you back to the game.

  • Feedback: A creaking sound when opening a door in Resident Evil, Blood splatters when players shoot something in Dead Space, or the sound of players' tools breaking in Minecraft are all examples of feedback being provided by the game developers to deepen the experience.

Conclusion

In conclusion, good utilisation of graphics and audio in video games are some of the building blocks that make up a good game. Game Developers and Designers should maintain the quality of these aspects to ensure an enjoyable experience for the players.

8. Development Process

Development process could be categorized into:[13]

  1. Pre-production
  2. Production
  3. Post-production

Pre-production

During pre-production, the concept of the game is formed. Developers will decide on:[14]

  • Game Genre:  What is the gameplay for the game? Some favourable game genres would include platformer game, shooter game, puzzle-solving games, survival games, party games, simulation game or strategy game. It's also time to determine if your game is single-player, local multiplayer or online game.

  • Core Gameplay Mechanics: What can the players do? Some controls to think about would be can the player jump, run, hide or shoot. What's is the goal of the players? Would you want the players to collect all coins, survive till the end or eliminate other?

  • Storyline and Characters: To ensure your game is interesting to the players, some questions you would be asking yourself during script writing, including how should the story progress, how many characters are there, how many characters are playable and how will the characters impact the storyline? Your story could be scary, romantic or comedy.

  • World Design: Maps design would change depending on the game. A open world would have a large area for the player to explore as they want. On the other hand, puzzle game would confined they players to a certain area. The theme of the map is also important, is it located in a desert, snowy mountain, forest or somewhere else?

  • Development Plan: How many members would you want to help develop this game, the more the member involved, the higher the expenses. When will the development start and end? When should we start testing the game? How does the development timetable look like These questions are ask to ensure your development progress is on time.

  • Target Demographic via Market Research: Set our target demographic, who would you like to play your game? The target demographic would impact how much we can charge for the game, if the target demographic is mostly young audience, the price should be too high. At the same time, researching competitors' game, this can determine how many active players can we expect and what have other competitors done with the game from the same genre.

Production

During production, developers will code for the game:[14]

  • Characters
  • In-game objects
  • Maps
  • Sound
  • UI

Developers will be the testers for the game during this time.

Post-production

After all the core mechanics are added into the game, developers will release the game as “alpha version” and “beta version” to the public for testing. During this time, developers will add additional features and optimize the game. Once the game is ready, it will be published and sold to the public. During post-production, developers will continue to update the game, regularly patching and fixing bugs to ensure the players’ gaming experience. Some developers could also start the production of the DLC(Downloadable Content) if the game is deemed successful.[13]

Conclusion

Developers should plan out the entire development process to streamline the process and ensure the game can be developed by the deadline.

9. Market Research

This process should be done during the pre-development of the game. It should let the developers know:[15]

  • The game target demographic
  • The potential income the game could generate
  • Whether it will be worth the developers’ time to spend in on development of the game
  • The competition the game will face

Knowing the competition the game will face allows the developers to identify hidden opportunities for their game and avoid competing in an oversaturated market. Discovering this early in the development process allows the developers to specialize their game genre or to switch to a different genre.

For example, MOBA games are over-saturated with the number of games published to the public, so developers could specialize the genre to be a first-person shooter MOBA game.

Developers could do surveys or gather existing data sources[16] to determine potential players for the game, and how the game should be headed to please the demographic. Developers could also analyse competitors’ games, using their weaknesses and strengths to improve the game.[17]

Conclusion

Market research is important because it tells the developers what the players want, how likely they will spend on the game and what feature the developers should add.[18] Ensuring the game could have a long and active player base.

  2. With the help of AI, indie game developers would be more favourable than big companies' game developers as they can do big projects while not having the stress big companies have (because they must rush deadlines and fear being fired) and able to work with flexible time.[25]
  3. Unreal engine currently uses a visual scripting called “Blueprint”. It allows new developers to create a simple game without writing a single code. This allows others to pick up game development as a hobby as in the future, writing code might no longer needed for creating a game.[26]
  4. Godot being completely free under the MIT license with no additional fees or contracts, giving creators the full rights to the game they build within the engine, can out-compete Unity or Unreal Engine that require developers to give some percentage of income from the game to them.[27]
  5. A completely open-source platform that runs on a few plug-in such as players movement and visual output will become popular. Players could fully customise their game by downloading plug-ins or moderation created by other developer. They could delete any features they don't like and add features they liked.
